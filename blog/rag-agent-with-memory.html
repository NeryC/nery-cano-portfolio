RAG from scratch: the guide I wish I'd had

This is an explicit guide, the kind I wish I had found. First what RAG is and why it exists, with diagrams. Then the decisions you will have to make and their alternatives. And at the end, the real code of an agent with memory I built and deployed. Read it top to bottom or jump to the part you are missing.

A language model knows an enormous amount about the world up to the date it was trained. About your company, your PDFs, your notes or your database, it knows nothing. And you cannot paste a 300-page manual into every question: it does not fit in the context window, it costs a fortune in tokens, and the model gets lost among so much irrelevant text.

RAG solves exactly that. Instead of feeding it everything, you find the few paragraphs that matter for that specific question and pass those alongside the query. The model answers with that evidence in front of it, cites where it came from, and if the fact is not there, you can make it say "I don't know" instead of inventing.

Five words, first

If you have never touched this, five terms are enough to follow everything that comes next. You do not need to know anything else to start.

  • Language model (LLM). The engine behind ChatGPT or Claude. It predicts text and knows a ton about the general world, but nothing about your private documents.
  • Token. A little piece of a word, the unit the model reads with and the unit you are billed in. "computer" is about two tokens; a page, about 500.
  • Context window. How much text the model can read at once. It is large, but not infinite, and everything you put in there you pay for on every question.
  • Vector (or embedding). A list of numbers that represents the meaning of a text. It is the central piece of RAG and I explain it in detail below; for now, think of it as the coordinates of an idea on a map.
  • Index. A structure that lets you search among millions of those vectors in milliseconds, like the index at the back of a book but for meanings.

With those five, you can follow the rest without getting lost.

What RAG is, in one paragraph

RAG stands for Retrieval-Augmented Generation. It is three moves. Retrieve: given a question, you search your knowledge base for the most relevant fragments. Augment: you paste those fragments into the prompt, next to the question. Generate: the model answers using that context as evidence. The whole trick is in the first step, in retrieving well; the rest is pasting text and calling the model.

Figure 1 · The RAG pipeline. Follow the token: the question is retrieved against the vector base, augments the prompt, and the model generates the answer with citations.

The problem RAG solves, and why not the other options

When you want a model to answer about data it did not see in training, you have four paths. RAG is one. It is worth understanding why it usually wins before building it.

Paste everything into the prompt. Simple, but it does not scale. A whole manual does not fit in the context window, and even if it did, you pay for every token on every query and the model gets distracted by the irrelevant. It works for a couple of pages, not for your knowledge base.

Fine-tuning. You retrain the model with your data. It changes the style and format of the answers far more than the factual knowledge, it is expensive to repeat every time your data changes, and it gives you no citations, no way to know where each claim came from. It is for teaching a tone or a task, not for "know the latest thing I uploaded."

Giant context window. New models accept hundreds of thousands of tokens. It helps, but it does not replace RAG: you still pay for everything you put in, latency rises, and accuracy drops when the relevant fact is buried in the middle of a huge text (the "lost in the middle" problem). RAG and long context combine well; they do not compete.

RAG. You bring only the fragments that matter, at question time. It is cheap (embedding and searching costs little), it updates instantly (you upload a document and it is queryable), and it gives you traceability: you know which paragraph backed each sentence. That last point, the citations, is what makes it usable for anything serious.

Rule of thumb

If the knowledge changes often and you need to know where each answer came from, it is RAG. If you want to teach it a style or a fixed task, it is fine-tuning. It is almost never "paste everything into the prompt," and long context is a complement to RAG, not a replacement.

The anatomy: the five steps

Every RAG system, however sophisticated, is a variation of five steps split across two moments. Three happen only once, at indexing time (when you upload a document). Two happen on every query. Separating those two moments in your head is half of understanding RAG.

Figure 2 · The two moments. You index once (top) and query many times (bottom). Both sides meet at the vector base, and use the same embedding function.

1 · Ingestion and splitting (chunking)

A whole document is too big to retrieve as one unit: if you bring back the entire PDF, you are back to the "paste everything" problem. So you cut it into fragments (chunks) of a few hundred words each. Each fragment has to be small enough to be precise and large enough to make sense on its own.

The detail almost everyone overlooks: the overlap. If you cut cleanly, an idea that falls right on the boundary gets split between two fragments and neither contains it whole. So each fragment carries the last sentences of the previous one. It costs a little storage and it avoids losing information at the seams.

Figure 3 · Chunking with overlap. Fragments share their edges so an idea straddling two of them is not lost.

2 · Embeddings: turning meaning into coordinates

This is the heart of RAG and the part that is hardest at first. An embedding is a function that takes a text and returns a list of numbers (a vector, say 1536 dimensions). The magic is that the function was trained so that texts with similar meaning end up close in that space, even if they share not a single word.

Think of it as a map: just as on a map nearby cities tend to share climate, on this "map of meanings" nearby texts share sense. "How much is shipping?" and "home delivery rates" have not one word in common, but they point to the same thing, so their vectors land close. Keyword search would not see the link; embedding search does. That is semantic search: you search by meaning, not by text match.

Figure 4 · The vector space. Each point is a fragment; similar ones cluster. Your question lands near its cluster and you retrieve its nearest neighbors.

You do not train this function yourself. You call an embeddings API (OpenAI, Voyage, Cohere) or run an open model. The golden rule: you must embed queries with the same model you embedded the documents with. Vectors from different models live in different spaces and cannot be compared.

3 · Storage and index

You now have one vector per fragment. Now you have to store them and, above all, find the ones closest to a query vector fast. Comparing against all of them one by one (brute force) works with thousands of vectors; with millions, it does not. That is where an approximate nearest neighbor index (ANN), like HNSW, comes in: it trades a bit of exactness for near-instant searches.

This lives in a vector database. It can be a dedicated one (Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant) or, as in my case, Postgres with the pgvector extension, which gives you vector search next to your normal tables without adding another piece of infrastructure. We compare the options below.

4 · Retrieval: cosine and top-k

A question arrives. You embed it with the same function, and now you have to measure which fragments are "close." The standard measure is cosine similarity: it looks at the angle between two vectors, not their size. Cosine 1 means same direction (same meaning); 0 means unrelated. You keep the top k (typically 3 to 8) and, optionally, discard those below a minimum threshold, so you do not drag in noise when nothing is truly relevant.

Figure 5 · Top-k by cosine. Order by similarity, cut by threshold, and only the best fragments enter the prompt.

5 · Generation with citations

The last step is the simplest. You build a prompt that says, in essence: "answer the question using only this context, and cite which document each claim comes from," you paste the retrieved fragments, and you call the model. Since each fragment knows which document and page it came from, the model can write [manual.pdf p.12] next to each fact. That citation is what separates a serious assistant from a plausible-text generator.

The strategies, in detail

The basic pipeline works, but each of its five steps has a fan of techniques behind it. Here is the full map, because "RAG does not work" is almost always one of these decisions made wrong, not the model. We go step by step, from simplest to most advanced.

Level heads-up

From here on we enter advanced territory. If you are just starting, everything above is enough to build a RAG that works; this is for when you want to squeeze it. I explain each technical term the first time it appears, and there is an express glossary of the acronyms at the end of the block.

Chunking strategies (much more than "cut every N")

How you cut the document decides how good retrieval can be: a badly cut fragment will never be retrieved well, no matter how good the search is. There is a whole ladder of strategies.

StrategyHow it worksWhen
Fixed sizeCuts every N characters or tokens, with overlapThe default. Simple and surprisingly hard to beat.
By sentenceGroups whole sentences until the fragment fillsAvoids cutting mid-sentence. Cheap to add.
RecursiveTries separators in order: paragraph, then sentence, then wordThe most used by libraries. Respects structure without configuring it.
By document structureCuts by Markdown, HTML or code headingsDocuments with clear hierarchy. Each fragment inherits its section.
SemanticEmbeds sentence by sentence and cuts where the topic shiftsWhen precision is worth the extra indexing cost.
Small-to-big / parentYou index small fragments but return the big paragraph containing themSearch precisely, answer with broad context. Very effective.
Contextual (Anthropic)Prepends a summary of where the fragment lives in the documentFragments that become ambiguous out of context ("it rose 3%": what?).
Late chunkingEmbeds the whole document and only then averages per fragmentEach fragment "knows" about the rest. Needs long-context models.

Figure 6 · The size tradeoff. Small wins precision, large wins context. The parent-document strategy keeps the best of both sides.

Embedding strategies

The model that turns text into a vector determines what "close" means. The options split by how they represent meaning and by where they run.

DimensionOptionsWhat to watch
Dense vs sparseDense (meaning) · sparse like SPLADE (terms)Dense ones catch synonyms; sparse ones, exact matches. Best is to combine them (hybrid).
ProviderOpenAI · Voyage · Cohere · open (BGE, E5)Fast-to-start API vs running your own model with zero cost per token.
Dimensions256 to 3072, some truncatable (Matryoshka)More dimensions, more nuance and more storage and index cost.
Domain and languageGeneral · technical · multilingualA model trained on your domain beats a general one even with fewer dimensions.

The one rule you cannot break: the same model to index and to query. Vectors from different models live in different spaces and do not compare.

Index strategies

How you store the vectors sets the tradeoff between exactness and speed.

IndexWhat it doesTradeoff
Flat (exact)Compares against all, one by onePerfect accuracy, but slow with millions of vectors.
IVFGroups into cells and searches only the near onesFast, with a bit of loss. Has to be trained.
HNSWA navigable graph of neighborsToday's standard: very fast and very accurate. Uses more memory. The one I use.

Retrieval strategies (the heart)

This is where RAG is won or lost, and where there are the most techniques. Top-k by cosine is the starting point, not the goal. Think of it as a funnel: you retrieve broad and cheap, and keep filtering with methods that get more precise and more expensive.

Figure 7 · The retrieval funnel. Many cheap candidates at the top, few precise fragments at the bottom. Each stage costs more and sharpens more.

TechniqueWhat it solves
Dense (semantic)The default: finds meaning even when the words do not match.
Sparse / BM25Exact matches of rare terms: codes, proper names, acronyms.
Hybrid + RRFRuns dense and sparse, and fuses both rankings with Reciprocal Rank Fusion. Almost always better than either alone.
RerankingYou pull 20 to 50 candidates and a cross-encoder reorders them looking at question and fragment at once. The highest-return improvement.
Query rewritingAn LLM reformulates the user's question into a better one for searching (resolves pronouns, adds context from the chat).
Multi-queryGenerates several versions of the question, searches with all, and merges. Covers different ways of saying the same thing.
HyDEGenerates a hypothetical answer and searches with that. Sometimes a fake answer resembles the real one more than the question does.
DecompositionBreaks a complex question into sub-questions, retrieves for each, and joins. For multi-hop queries.
Metadata filterRestricts by author, date, type or (in my case) session, before ordering by similarity.
MMR (diversity)Avoids bringing five fragments that say the same thing; balances relevance with variety.
Contextual retrievalStores each fragment with a summary of its context, so it does not become ambiguous when retrieved alone.
GraphRAGBuilds a graph of entities and relations, and retrieves by connections. For questions that cross many documents.
Express glossary of acronyms

BM25: classic keyword search. RRF: a simple formula to fuse two result lists into one. Cross-encoder: a model that reads question and fragment together to score how well they answer each other (slower and more precise than embedding them separately). MMR: a method to keep results from repeating each other. HyDE: generate a fake trial answer and search with it. HNSW / IVF: two ways to organize the vectors for fast search. nDCG / MRR / recall@k: three ways to grade how good the search was.

Generation strategies

The last step looks trivial, but it has decisions too. Grounding: the prompt has to order it to answer only with the given context, not with the model's general knowledge. Citations: if each fragment carries its origin, the model can write [file.pdf p.3] and make every sentence verifiable. Honest refusal: you have to give it explicit permission to say "this is not in your documents" instead of inventing; without that instruction, a helpful model fills the gap. That "I don't know" is as valuable as a good answer.

How to know if it works: evaluation

Here is the step almost everyone skips, and it is the one that separates a demo from a system. You cannot improve what you do not measure, and "it feels like it answers well" is not measuring. RAG is evaluated in two halves, because it has two parts that can fail separately.

Retrieval is measured with a set of questions where you already know which fragment should come back (a golden set). With that you compute recall@k (is the right fragment among the k you brought?), MRR (how high up did it appear?) and nDCG (is the order good?). Generation is measured differently: faithfulness (does the answer rest only on the context, or did it hallucinate?), relevance (does it answer what was asked?) and citation accuracy. Tools like RAGAS automate much of this using an LLM as judge.

The same principle as always

A golden set of twenty questions with their expected fragment turns "I changed the chunking, did it improve?" from a hunch into a number. It is exactly the evals discipline I used in the harness engineering series: without a way to measure, every change is blind faith.

The most common failure modes

SymptomAlmost always the cause
Brings irrelevant fragmentsBad chunking, or the wrong embedding model for your domain.
The fact exists but it does not find itMissing hybrid search (rare term) or the fragment got split.
Hallucinates with the context right thereThe prompt does not force grounding, or you put in too many fragments and it got lost in the middle.
Cites the source wrongThe origin metadata does not travel with the fragment to the prompt.
Good in tests, bad in productionThere was never a golden set; you tested with the questions you already knew worked.
The order that pays

Get the full basic pipeline working before adding hybrid, reranking or contextual. Most "RAG does not work" cases are the two most boring things: badly cut fragments or the wrong embedding model. Measure first, optimize later.

The minimal RAG, in code

With the theory clear, the whole pipeline fits on one screen. This is the essence, no frills: index and query.

// --- INDEX (once per document) ---
const chunks = split(text, { tokens: 500, overlap: 50 })
for (const group of inBatches(chunks, 100)) {
  const vectors = await embed(group)             // same function as at query time
  await db.insert("chunks", group, vectors)      // stores text + vector + metadata
}

// --- QUERY (on every question) ---
const q = await embed([question])
const top = await db.rpc("match_chunks", {       // nearest neighbors by cosine
  query_embedding: q, match_count: 5, match_threshold: 0.25,
})
const prompt = `Answer using only this context and cite the source:
${top.map(c => `[${c.filename} p.${c.page}] ${c.content}`).join("\n\n")}

Question: ${question}`
const answer = await model.generate(prompt)      // the LLM answers with citations

Everything else (agents, memory, streaming, reranking) is layers on top of these twelve lines. If you understand this, you understand RAG.

The pattern, generalized

RAG is a simpler idea than its name suggests: give the model the right evidence at the right time, instead of hoping it already knows. Everything else is engineering detail around that idea.

And once you see it that way, it shows up everywhere. Documents are RAG. An agent's memory is RAG over what you remember. Web search is RAG with an index you do not control. The mechanism (embed, search by closeness, inject) is always the same, and the hard part is always the same: retrieving well. If your answers are bad, it is almost never the fault of the model doing the generating; it is that you are giving it the wrong context. Fix retrieval and you fix RAG.
