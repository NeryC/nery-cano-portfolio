Harness Engineering: how to design repos where AI doesn't screw up

Guides, sensors, context, evals and autonomy limits: the harness that turns an AI agent from a chaotic intern into a reliable colleague. Spoiler: the problem is your repo, not the model.

Nery Cano
Senior Full-Stack Engineer · AI/LLM Systems · github.com/NeryC

This is the first of two parts. Here I explain what harness engineering is and how you build one, from scratch. In part 2 I show you how I applied it to a real multi-tenant SaaS in production, with a baseline, diffs, and a couple of embarrassing moments. Let's start with the theory, which in this case is surprisingly practical.

You ask your AI agent to fix a one-line bug. It comes back with fourteen modified files, a README nobody asked for, a refactor thrown in for free, and (just in case) it deleted two tests that were getting in its way. Everything passes. You merge it. Monday, a user tells you the app is on fire. Sound familiar?

Coding agents aren't a toy anymore: they write real code, fast enough to be unsettling. The problem is how they write it. They work like a brilliant intern on their first day: fast, unshakably confident, and with zero idea of the unwritten rules of your house. That the email field is never exposed to that role? They didn't know. That the function already exists in another module? They rewrote it. That those tests are load-bearing? They were an obstacle to green, so they're gone.

The instinctive reaction is to fight the model: longer prompts, more "please," more capital letters. It doesn't work, and that's the clue that we're looking in the wrong place.

The thesis

You will not fix an agent by asking it to behave. You fix it by designing the environment it works in: the instructions it reads, the tests that catch it, the boundaries it can't cross. That discipline is called harness engineering, and it's increasingly a role of its own.

This isn't a blog trend

Before we go on, a reality check for the skeptics (I was one). In February 2026, a team at OpenAI described building and shipping an internal product with zero hand-written lines of code: on the order of a million lines, ~1,500 pull requests, with a team of three to seven engineers. Their stated conclusion? That their hardest challenges are no longer writing code, but "designing environments, feedback loops, and control systems."

It's not an isolated case. Anthropic publishes about "effective harnesses for long-running agents." At Thoughtworks, Birgitta Böckeler writes the conceptual framework we're going to use here. And AGENTS.md, the instruction file for agents, became an open standard across OpenAI, Google, Cursor and company in August 2025. When companies that compete with each other agree on a file format, something real is happening.

The good news: you don't need a million lines or seven engineers to benefit. The principles scale down. Let's get to them.

The core idea: Agent = Model + Harness

Here's the mental shift everything else follows from. A coding agent is not "the model." It's the model plus everything around it: the system prompt, the tools it can call, the context injected into it, the tests that run, the limits on what it can touch. That whole wrapper is called the harness.

The formula, borrowed from LangChain, is simple: Agent = Model + Harness. And it has a liberating consequence. You don't control the model: another company trained it, it cost hundreds of millions, and you won't improve it from your laptop. But the harness is entirely yours. It's the only lever you can pull.

Figure 1 · The only ring you control. It's the outer one, and it's enough.

Notice the trap in the language: when someone says "the agent hallucinated" or "the agent is bad," they almost always mean the model. But the model is the layer you can't change. The useful question isn't "how do I get a better model?" but "what is my harness missing that forces this model to guess?" That reframe is the job.

In one sentence

Harness engineering is designing the environment (documentation, tools, feedback and constraints) so that an agent does reliable work with minimal human supervision.

The mental map: two axes, four quadrants

If you take one diagram away from this post, take this one. Every control you put in a harness lands somewhere on a two-axis grid. Memorize it and you've understood most of this.

The first axis is when the control acts:

  • Guides (feedforward): they orient the agent before it writes a line. They raise the odds it gets it right the first time.
  • Sensors (feedback): they observe after it acted and let it correct itself. They catch what the guide didn't anticipate.

The second axis is how it works:

  • Computational: deterministic, instant, dirt cheap. A test, a linter, a type-checker. Never hallucinates.
  • Inferential: uses another model to judge with semantic taste. It understands what you meant, but it's slow, costs money, and sometimes makes things up.

Figure 2 · Two axes, four quadrants. Guides orient before (feedforward); sensors catch after (feedback).

From this comes the golden rule: always prefer the computational quadrant when one exists. A test that runs in milliseconds and returns a reliable answer is worth more than an expensive LLM judge that sometimes invents. The inferential is reserved for what no test can judge: is this over-engineered? did it misread the request? "we don't do it that way here"?

And the second lesson, equally important: you need both rows. Guides only, and you get an agent that encodes rules but never learns whether they worked. Sensors only, and you get an agent that trips over the same rock forever. The value is in combining them.

Reality check

Yes, technically you could put an LLM in charge of reviewing every comma of every commit. You could also hire someone to read your typos out loud. One of those two ideas costs less. Neither is a good idea.

The loop: orient → act → catch → correct

Guides and sensors aren't two loose lists: they form a control loop. The guide orients, the agent acts, the sensor measures. Then the useful part: the sensor's output goes back to the agent so it corrects itself, before you see anything.

Figure 3 · The steering loop. Follow the token: the sensor's output travels back into the agent's context, and it carries the repair instruction with it.

The best trick in this loop is subtle. A good sensor injects the repair instruction into the agent's context instead of only reporting the error. A linter message that says "use X instead of Y, like this: …" is feedback optimized for the model to fix itself. Böckeler calls it, with a smile, "prompt injection, but the good kind."

And the human? The role changes completely. You no longer write the code: you steer the loop. Every time a problem shows up twice, you improve a guide or a sensor so the next time is less likely. That's the steering loop, and it's the difference between a living harness and a pile of dead configs.

How you build a harness

Theory so far. Now the concrete part: what, exactly, goes in that outer ring? Five pieces. Each fills a different hole, and together they're the system.

01

Context

So it doesn't have to guess: instructions, docs and schemas the agent reads.

02

Sensors

So it catches itself: tests, linters, type-checkers, hooks.

03

Evals

Measure whether the agent reliably gets a class of tasks right.

04

Autonomy

What it always does, what it asks about first, what it never touches.

05

Traces

Observability: being able to see what the agent did and why, to debug and improve the loop.

Figure 4 · The five components. None is optional; all are incremental.

01 · Context: so it doesn't have to guess

The principle that organizes this whole component was summed up by OpenAI in one brutal line: "what the agent cannot see does not exist." That architectural decision you made in a meeting, that "here the doormen do see X" that lives in your head, that implicit API contract: for the agent, none of it exists unless it's written in a file it can read.

In practice, three things: a short AGENTS.md that works as an index (not an encyclopedia; a 1,000-line manual buries what matters), a docs/ directory that's the system of record for decisions and plans, and schemas generated from the code (the database, the API contract) so the agent never infers the shape of a payload. Every time the agent guesses, you introduce a class of bug. Context is what removes the guessing.

02 · Sensors: so it catches itself

This is where tests, linters, type-checkers and hooks live. But the most valuable sensor is the one that turns a convention into a rule that fails on its own. Your layered architecture is just a nice habit until an import test breaks the build when someone crosses a layer. "Sensitive fields aren't exposed by default" is a wish until a test verifies it on every serialization.

The highest-return tactic is called shift-left: run the cheap stuff as early as possible (a pre-commit hook that takes a second) instead of waiting for CI or, worse, production. The earlier you catch it, the cheaper it is.

The rule that costs the most to learn

A sensor that lies is worse than no sensor. A flaky test, a check that passes for the wrong reason, a metric that doesn't measure what you think: they give you false confidence, and false confidence makes you merge with a smile. Before adding new sensors, make sure the ones you have aren't lying to you.

03 · Evals: measuring whether it gets it right

This is the distinction most people confuse, so let's go slowly. A test verifies the code: given this input, this output; pass or fail. An eval verifies the agent: given this kind of task, does it reliably produce a good result? It's measured as a rate, 8 out of 10, not as green or red.

Why does it matter? Because without evals, every change you make to your AGENTS.md is blind faith: you don't know whether it improved or degraded the agent. With a small set of representative tasks and a rubric, the steering loop stops being intuition and becomes measurable. Change a guide, re-run the evals, see if the rate went up. That's engineering; the other thing is astrology.

Intellectual honesty

Evals reduce uncertainty; they don't prove correctness. The "behavioral harness", guaranteeing the app functionally does the right thing, is still unsolved across the industry. Anyone selling you total certainty is selling you something.

04 · Autonomy: always, ask, never

The most underrated component, and the one that saves you from disaster. It consists of declaring, explicitly, what the agent can do without permission, what it has to check first, and what it must never touch. Without this table, the agent "helps" by touching a database migration on a Friday at 6pm, with the best intentions in the world.

ALWAYS
  • Add tests
  • Fix broken tests
  • Refactor within a layer
  • Formatting / lint fixes
ASK FIRST
  • New endpoint or page
  • Schema change / migration
  • New dependency
  • Touching permissions / auth
NEVER
  • Migrate data in production
  • Touch PII / encryption
  • Delete data
  • Push directly to main

Figure 5 · The autonomy table. Tune it to your blast radius.

And it goes hand in hand with a ladder: you start on the lowest rung (the agent opens a PR, you review and merge) and climb one step only when the sensors stop surprising you. Don't skip rungs out of enthusiasm; each step is earned when the harness gives you measured confidence, not vibes.

Three things make that ladder real, and all three are easy to skip. Write the promotion criterion before you want to climb. If you write it the day you're itching to remove yourself from the merge path, you'll write one you already pass. Enforce the scope in code, not in a document: "auto-merge only touches peripheral files" is a wish until the workflow reads the diff and refuses. And have a documented way back down before you go up: a per-change opt-out, a global kill-switch, and a written trigger that sends you back to the previous rung.

Figure 6 · The ladder. Each rung is earned by a criterion written before you want to climb. Watch the marker cross the gate anyway.

The one that bites

The harness governs the agent, because the agent reads it. It does not govern you, because you wrote it. Every rule you care about (the promotion criterion, the merge scope, the success rate you swore you'd compute) has to live somewhere that executes, or it becomes decoration.

05 · Traces: being able to see what happened

Last: observability. Two layers. The first is making runtime observability legible to the agent, so it can query logs and metrics during a task, not just you afterward in a dashboard. The second is traces of the agent's trajectory: prompts, tool calls, diffs. That's what lets you answer "why on earth did it do that?" when something goes sideways, and feed the steering loop with data instead of hunches.

Don't build it all at once

The classic mistake, now that you've seen all five pieces, is wanting to build them in one weekend. Don't. A harness is cultivated, one piece at a time, and each piece earns its place by proving it saves you time.

Figure 7 · One piece per lap. If it doesn't move the number, it doesn't belong.

Measure a baseline first (how the agent does with nothing). Add the guides layer and measure again. Then the sensors, and measure again. If a piece doesn't move the number, remove it. Not every repo needs everything. This approach, besides saving you weeks, is what separates a harness engineer with judgment from one who collected tools because they saw them in a blog post.

The traps (because it isn't magic)

To close, the four things that trip people up most easily:

  • Cargo cult. OpenAI had a throughput problem and seven engineers. You might be one. Don't copy their full infrastructure; copy the principles and size them to your scale.
  • The lying sensor. We said it already, and we're repeating it because it's expensive: trusting a check that doesn't measure what you think is worse than flying blind.
  • Confusing "green tests" with "correct." An agent can write the code and the test that armors its own wrong decision. Green doesn't protect you from judgment.
  • Believing the human is redundant. The harness doesn't eliminate your judgment; it concentrates it where it's worth most. You're still the one steering.

The pattern, generalized

When does all of this apply? The moment you let an agent touch your repo with any autonomy, you're already doing harness engineering. The only question is whether you're doing it on purpose or by accident. You don't need to be aiming at "a million lines with no human code" for it to pay off. A hundred-line AGENTS.md, two sensors that catch your most frequent mistakes, and an autonomy table already change your relationship with the tool: from praying over every PR, to trusting the system around it.

Because the line we opened with was a trap. The failure lives in the gap between you and the model: everything you knew and never wrote down. Harness engineering is filling that gap on purpose.
